# Deliverables Guide: Insights, Readouts, Repositories, Portfolios

Reference file for the applied-practice skill. Loaded when formulating
insights, building client readouts and reports, structuring research
repositories, and writing portfolio case studies.

---

## Insight Formulation

The atomic unit of applied deliverables is the insight, and it has an
anatomy:

1. **Claim** — an interpreted pattern, stated as a sentence someone
   could disagree with. "Nurses treat the scheduling system as a
   negotiation record, not a calendar" — not "users have workarounds."
2. **Evidence** — verbatim quotes and observed moments, with
   provenance (role, site, session), chosen to complicate as well as
   confirm. This is thick description in its applied form: enough
   context that the reader briefly inhabits the participant's logic.
3. **Implication** — what this means for the decision on the table,
   stated in the client's terms.

Distinguish ruthlessly between findings (what was observed), insights
(what it means), and recommendations (what to do) — collapsing them is
how research gets dismissed as opinion.

Quote ethics in client contexts: attribute to roles or personas, never
names; verify the quote is verbatim; never composite multiple people
into one "participant" without labeling it a composite; check consent
scope before a quote appears in any deck that travels.

## The Readout

The structural arc, in order:

| Component | Job | Craft notes |
|---|---|---|
| Executive summary | The whole story in two minutes | One page maximum; leads with what changes; survives being the only page read |
| Business framing | The decisions this research informs | Names the questions as revised at the reframing checkpoint |
| Method note | Credibility without jargon | Two paragraphs: who, where, how long, how analyzed; rigor signaled by specificity, not vocabulary |
| Thematic insights | The core: 3–6 insights | One insight per section: claim, verbatim evidence, implication; field photos only within consent scope |
| Strategic implications | Pattern → position | What the insights mean for the client's competitive and organizational reality; where the evidence supports it, project forward and mark where extrapolation begins |
| Recommendations | Commitments, not suggestions | Specific, sequenced, owned — each names a team and a next step |

Register rules: scannable and modular (a reader entering at any
section can orient); active voice; the interpretive depth carried by
the evidence and the claims, not by theoretical vocabulary. The
anthropology is in what the deck sees, not what it cites.

The written report (when contracted) extends the same arc with the
fuller evidence base and an appendix trail back to the data —
provenance is the applied audit trail.

## The Research Repository

Scaled practice outlives projects through repositories of atomic,
tagged findings rather than shelved decks:

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| Atomic finding | One synthesized statement of behavior, pain point, or cultural driver |
| Evidence link | Transcript passage, clip, or fieldnote it derives from |
| Source and method | Study, date, method, site — so future readers can judge context |
| Tags | Controlled vocabulary (journey stage, product area, population) |
| Consent scope | What reuse the participant agreed to — the field most schemas omit and the one that matters most |
| Decisions informed | What the organization did because of this finding |

Two anthropological obligations the standard ResearchOps schema
misses:

- **Consent travels with the datum.** A story shared for a product
  study is not thereby available for sales decks, marketing, or model
  training. Record scope; enforce it at reuse time.
- **Context is part of the finding.** Atomization strips the
  circumstances that made the observation meaningful; the evidence
  link is not optional metadata but the finding's tether to reality.
  A repository of context-free assertions is a rumor mill with tags.

## The Portfolio Case Study

Practicing careers are built on case studies, not publication lists.
The genre (one page to a few pages per case):

1. **The challenge** — the client's situation and the decision at
   stake (anonymized to industry and scale as NDAs require).
2. **The approach** — what was done and why ethnography fit: what the
   method could see that analytics and surveys could not.
3. **The discovery** — the central insight, with one telling detail
   that only fieldwork would have caught.
4. **The outcome** — what the client decided or changed, and any
   measurable result; where impact is unattributable, say what shipped.

NDA discipline: anonymize clients unless cleared; never trade
confidentiality for portfolio credibility. The venue for methods-level
sharing is the practitioner literature — EPIC's proceedings exist
precisely so practicing anthropologists can publish craft without
breaching clients (see, e.g., Madsen, Munk, and Søltoft 2023);
practitioner career paths and genre expectations are mapped in Artz
and Koycheva (2024).
