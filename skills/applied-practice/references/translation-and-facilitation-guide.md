# Translation and Facilitation Guide

Reference file for the applied-practice skill. Loaded when translating
anthropological concepts for business audiences, reading engagement
friction, and designing alignment workshops.

---

## Semantic Translation — With the Anthropology Intact

Business audiences experience academic vocabulary as abstraction;
practitioners translate. But translation is a register change, not an
epistemology change: each business term below is the *packaging*, and
the anthropological content is what makes it worth buying. The third
column is the discipline's quality bar — what must survive the
translation.

| Anthropological concept | Business register | What must survive |
|---|---|---|
| Emic perspective | Latent user needs; mental models | The account stays in the participants' own logic — not the team's assumptions restated as "needs" |
| Etic framework | Market dynamics; external context | Comparison is structured, not vibes: the frame is named and applied consistently |
| Thick description | Context-rich qualitative data | Enough circumstance that behavior makes sense from inside; a quote with its situation, not a soundbite |
| Participant observation | Contextual inquiry; immersive research | Presence over time in the participant's setting — not a 30-minute moderated session relabeled |
| Reflexivity | Bias mitigation; quality control | The researcher's position and the sponsor's stakes are actually examined, not checkboxed |
| Ritual and symbolism | Habits; routines; brand meaning | The meaning-carrying character of the practice — why it matters, not just that it recurs |
| Structural constraint | Systemic friction; churn drivers | Causes located in systems and power, not repackaged as individual user failure |

Working rules:

- Translate at the surface, cite in the appendix: the deck says
  "latent needs," the analysis that produced it was emic, and the
  practitioner can defend the chain when asked.
- Never let the business term redefine the work backwards (the
  "contextual inquiry" that shrinks to a usability session; the
  "mental model" that means the PM's flowchart).
- Borrowed frameworks are entry points, not method: jobs-to-be-done
  gives teams a handy grammar, but it models people as rational
  hirers of products — supplement it with the cultural and relational
  context it strips out, which is precisely the anthropological
  contribution (Sunderland and Denny 2014).
- Theory works best in applied settings as quick, provisional models
  assembled from multiple sources and iterated against the field — a
  deliberately low-ceremony way of using the discipline's ideas
  without importing its seminar voice.

## Friction as Signal

Engagement friction is data about the organization — the applied
cousin of ethnographic disconcertment. Read it before smoothing it:

| Friction | What it usually marks | Move |
|---|---|---|
| Unexpected resistance to the research | Fear of what findings imply for roles or turf | Reframe as partnership; find what the resister is protecting — it is often the real topic |
| Emotional responses in sessions | Something at stake that surveys never surfaced | Slow down; open-ended follow-up; treat the heat as a pointer, not a derailment |
| "Your data contradicts our metrics" | Epistemological split: what each side counts as knowing | Triangulate rather than argue — pair the observation with the analytics and interpret the gap itself |
| Stakeholder skepticism of qualitative rigor | Unfamiliarity with the method's warrant | Show the chain: evidence → claim → implication; specificity persuades where vocabulary alienates |
| The same concern recurring across sessions | A systemic issue, not an individual complaint | Elevate it to a theme even if it was nobody's research question |
| Communication breakdown between functions | Vocabulary worlds (design, eng, sales) that do not share referents | Facilitate translation explicitly — glossaries and artifacts, not louder repetition |

## Workshop Facilitation

Workshops convert findings into commitments; the facilitator's craft
is participation-structure work — managing voice, status, and
turn-taking in a room, which anthropologists are trained to see.

### The arc

1. **Frame** — the insights in the room (pre-read or 10-minute
   replay), the decision to be made, the definition of done.
2. **Diverge** — generate widely before judging. Use silent,
   written-first formats (each participant writes ideas before any
   discussion; rounds build on neighbors' sheets) so the loudest
   voice does not set the frame and junior people contribute before
   hierarchy activates.
3. **Converge** — sort and prioritize against explicit criteria
   (impact for users, feasibility, evidence strength). Sorting
   matrices beat open debate: the criteria argue so people don't.
4. **Commit** — every surviving item gets an owner and a next step in
   the room. Unowned items are recorded as explicitly parked, not
   vaguely alive.

### Facilitation as applied anthropology

- Watch participation structure: who speaks after whom, who gets
  interrupted, whose ideas are re-voiced by someone senior and
  credited to the re-voicer. Design against it (written formats,
  round-robins, anonymous clustering) rather than refereeing it live.
- The workshop is also fieldwork: how the organization behaves in the
  room — what it can and cannot say to itself — is evidence for the
  engagement.
- Ground exercises in the research: affinity-cluster real quotes, map
  the observed journey (not the official one), stress-test roadmap
  ideas against the personas' worlds. Generic ideation exercises
  without the field material produce the same workshop every
  consultancy runs.
