# Engagement Scoping Guide

Reference file for the applied-practice skill. Loaded when scoping a
client engagement: choosing a statement-of-work structure, defining
deliverables and responsibilities, and protecting both the research and
the relationship.

---

## Matching SOW Structure to Research Reality

The central scoping decision is honesty about how much is knowable up
front. Ethnographic engagements sit at different points on that curve,
and the contract type must match:

| SOW type | Structure | Fits ethnographic work when... |
|---|---|---|
| Time-and-materials | Hourly/daily rates, flexible scope | The work is genuinely exploratory — parameters cannot be fixed before fieldwork begins |
| Level-of-effort | Time and presence, not fixed artifacts | Embedded advisory roles, ongoing research partnership, coaching an internal team |
| Design (prescriptive) | Exact specifications and process | Multi-site evaluation with a stable protocol — uniform guides, fixed capture parameters |
| Performance-based | Outcomes, execution freedom | Evaluation against measurable outcomes (retention, adoption) where the client cares about results, not method |
| Hybrid | Phased combination | The common real case: exploratory discovery phase (T&M) feeding a fixed-deliverable synthesis phase |

Forcing exploratory work into a prescriptive SOW is the structural
version of the wrong research question: it contracts away the method's
value before the work begins.

## The Five Load-Bearing Sections

1. **Project summary** — one paragraph: the organizational challenge
   and why this engagement, now. Written for the executive who reads
   nothing else.
2. **Deliverables** — the specific artifacts (readout deck, written
   report, repository entries, workshop, raw-data handling terms),
   each with enough specification to be recognizable on arrival.
3. **Timeline and milestones** — phases with dates, including
   fieldwork windows that acknowledge recruitment reality.
4. **Client responsibilities** — the section most often omitted and
   the leading cause of stalled engagements. Name what the client owes:
   site and system access, participant recruitment support or
   permission, stakeholder introductions, review turnaround times
   (with a default: "feedback within N business days or the milestone
   ships as delivered").
5. **Success benchmarks** — criteria the method can honestly meet:
   the decisions informed, the risks surfaced, the options generated,
   coverage achieved. Not "statistically significant findings" from a
   study designed for depth.

Add change terms: what happens when scope moves — because in
ethnographic engagements, it moves.

## The Reframing Checkpoint

Applied ethnography's most valuable and most disruptive output is the
discovery that the commissioning question is the wrong question — the
client asked about feature adoption and the field said the problem is
trust between shifts. Contract for this:

- Schedule a **midpoint reframing session** in the SOW: findings so
  far, and an explicit agenda item — "does the research question still
  hold?"
- Frame revision as engagement value ("the research corrected our aim
  while there was still budget to act on it"), not as drift.
- Record the revised question in writing; it becomes the readout's
  business framing.

This is the contractual form of a disciplinary habit: ethnographers
expect the field to push back on the frame (Cefkin 2009; Ladner 2014).

## Scoping the Relationship, Not Just the Work

- **Access is a research condition.** If the engagement needs shop
  floors, patient areas, or internal tools, the SOW says so — access
  failures discovered in week three are scope failures.
- **Consent and data terms belong in the contract.** Who owns raw
  field materials, what the client may reuse (see the repository
  consent-scope guidance in the deliverables guide), what gets
  destroyed and when, and the practitioner's obligation to protect
  participants — including from the client. AAA ethical principles do
  not distinguish academic from applied work.
- **Unwelcome findings clause, in spirit if not in letter:** the
  practitioner reports what the field shows, including "don't build
  it." An engagement priced only for good news is not research.
- **The B2B split.** When the buyer and the end user differ, scope
  research for both worlds explicitly — the purchasing logic and the
  daily-use logic — or say plainly which one the budget covers.

## Estimating Honestly

- Recruitment is the schedule risk: specialized participants,
  gatekeepers, and consent processes take longer than clients expect.
  Estimate recruitment as its own line, not as friction inside
  fieldwork.
- Synthesis time is analysis, not admin — budget it at parity with
  fieldwork for interpretive work, and defend it: the synthesis is
  what the client is buying.
- Rapid variants (days-scale sprints) shrink the question and the
  sample, not the standards; say what a sprint can and cannot claim.
