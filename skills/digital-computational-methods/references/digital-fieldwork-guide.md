# Digital Fieldwork Guide

Reference file for the digital-computational-methods skill. Loaded when
designing digital ethnography or netnography — fieldwork in and through
online spaces — including site selection, presence, capture, and
platform ethics.

The tradition: netnography (Kozinets 2010) and digital ethnography
(Pink et al. 2016) extend interpretive fieldwork into digitally mediated
life. The digital is at once field site and condition — a place where
culture is made and a medium that saturates the rest of experience
(Horst and Miller 2012; Geismar and Knox 2021). The stance stays
ethnographic: sustained attention, relational access, meaning over
metrics.

---

## Site Selection

- Select by where the phenomenon lives, not by data convenience. "This
  subreddit exports easily" is not a rationale; "this is where the
  community's decision-making actually happens" is.
- Document the boundary decisions: which platforms, which spaces within
  them (a server, a hashtag, a group), which period, and what was
  deliberately excluded.
- Expect multi-sited reality: communities span platforms (public forum +
  private chat + video), and following the phenomenon may mean following
  it across.
- Distinguish space types, because obligations differ:

| Space | Examples | Design implications |
|---|---|---|
| Public broadcast | public posts, public video, open forums | observation defensible; consent still needed for quoting identifiable members in sensitive contexts |
| Gated community | Discord servers, private groups, invite forums | entry requires permission; announced researcher identity is the default |
| Ephemeral/semi-private | stories, disappearing messages, live chats | capture itself is an ethical decision; members reasonably expect impermanence |

## Presence and Access

- Choose a presence stance and justify it: observation only
  ("lurking"), participation, or announced researcher identity.
  Sustained unannounced observation of a gated community is rarely
  defensible; announced presence changes the field, and that change is
  data, not contamination.
- Negotiate entrée with moderators/admins as gatekeepers — they are
  also informants about the community's own norms.
- Build relationships the same way as offline fieldwork: reciprocity,
  presence over time, sensitivity to internal hierarchies (regulars,
  newcomers, moderators).

## Capture and Data Management

- Decide in advance what is recorded: threads, posts, screenshots,
  media, metadata (timestamps, usernames, reactions), and your own
  interactions. Screenshots preserve context (layout, replies,
  reactions) that text export strips.
- Handle deletion honestly: if a member deletes a post, the polite
  default is that it leaves the corpus too — state the policy in the
  protocol.
- Preserve permalinks and capture dates; platforms redesign and content
  moves.
- Trace data (what people leave behind) answers behavioral questions;
  elicited data (interviews, walkthroughs, media diaries) answers
  meaning questions. Most designs need both — a scraped corpus cannot
  say what participation feels like.
- Fieldnotes still carry the analysis: observational jottings, expanded
  notes, and memos apply online exactly as offline (the
  fieldwork-methods skill covers note systems).

## Platform Ethics

- **Publicness is not consent** (franzke et al. 2020). Visibility makes
  observation possible, not automatically ethical. Weigh member
  expectations: a public post addressed to a small community is not
  offered to research.
- **Terms of service bind the method.** Scraping restrictions, API
  terms, and rate limits are design constraints; violating them
  endangers participants' platforms and the research alike. Where terms
  block necessary collection, redesign (elicitation, donation of data by
  members, platform partnership) rather than quietly violate.
- **Identifiability outlives anonymization.** Verbatim quotes from
  public posts are searchable back to their authors; paraphrase,
  aggregate, or obtain consent for quoting. Usernames are identities —
  treat them as names.
- **Vulnerable communities online are still vulnerable.** Stigmatized
  health conditions, legal precarity, harassment targets: heightened
  obligations follow the people, not the platform.
- Route formal instruments through the ethics skills: protocol
  narratives → irb-protocol; consent design (including how consent is
  sought in chat-based communities) → informed-consent.
