# Framework Construction Guide

Reference file for the literature-review skill. Loaded when moving from
synthesized literature to the theoretical or conceptual framework that
will govern the study.

---

## Theoretical vs. Conceptual Framework

The distinction matters because it determines what the researcher must
build versus adopt:

- **Theoretical framework**: an established theory (or a small set in
  deliberate combination) adopted to interpret the phenomenon. It
  supplies the concepts, the questions worth asking, and the standards
  of evidence. The researcher's task is *justified adoption*: why this
  theory, for this phenomenon, in this setting — and what the theory's
  known blind spots mean for the design.
- **Conceptual framework**: the researcher's own synthesis, built when
  no single theory covers the study's terrain. It assembles concepts
  from multiple literatures and commits to proposed relationships among
  them. The researcher's task is *construction with provenance*: every
  concept and every proposed relationship traces to sources in the
  matrix, and the parts that are the study's own contribution are
  labeled as such.

In anthropological practice the theoretical framework is also a
*conversation choice* — adopting practice theory versus political
economy versus an ontological approach decides whose questions the
study inherits and which debates it must answer to. The toolkit's
canonical lens list (skills/DESIGN.md) is a useful inventory of the
candidate orientations; the analytical-lens machinery in the
qualitative-analysis pipeline operationalizes whichever the framework
adopts.

## Construction in Three Movements

### I. Prepare

1. **Delimit the phenomenon.** State what the framework must explain
   and what lies outside it. An undelimited framework grows until it
   explains nothing.
2. **Gather and synthesize.** The completed literature matrix is the
   input — frameworks built from memory of the reading inherit its
   biases.

### II. Construct

3. **Extract the working concepts.** From the matrix's key-concepts
   column, list the terms that actually do analytical work across
   sources; merge synonyms, split false friends (the same word doing
   different work in different literatures).
4. **Map the relationships.** For each pair of concepts the study
   cares about, commit: does one shape, enable, constrain, mediate, or
   moderate the other — or are they rival descriptions of the same
   thing? In variable-oriented (applied/mixed-methods) projects, this
   is the independent/dependent/mediator/moderator mapping; in
   interpretive projects, it is the claim structure — what the
   framework says produces, sustains, or transforms what.
5. **Model at the right abstraction.** State the framework compactly —
   a diagram plus a paragraph per relationship. Variable-oriented
   designs may formalize (a mediation or moderation structure);
   interpretive designs state the relationships as claims precise
   enough to be argued with.
6. **Align with the empirical plan.** Every concept needs an empirical
   face: what would be observed, asked, or coded to see it. A concept
   with no empirical face belongs in the discussion section, not the
   framework.

### III. Consolidate

7. **Verify internal consistency.** Each research question maps to
   framework elements; no element is orphaned; no two relationships
   contradict each other without the tension being named as part of
   the design.
8. **Render and narrate.** Produce the visual (a simple boxes-and-
   arrows diagram — Mermaid renders well in the toolkit's contexts)
   and the narrative walk-through. The diagram makes commitments
   visible; the narrative defends them from the literature.

## Quality Checks

- **Provenance**: every element traces to matrix rows or is explicitly
  marked as the study's proposed contribution.
- **Falsifiability-in-spirit**: even interpretive frameworks should be
  precise enough that fieldwork could complicate them; a framework no
  data could disturb is decoration.
- **Economy**: the framework earns each concept; cut anything that
  neither shapes a question nor guides collection or analysis.
- **The lens is declared**: the theoretical orientation is stated as a
  commitment, not smuggled in as neutral description.

## Common Confusions

- **Literature review ≠ framework.** The review surveys the
  conversation; the framework takes a position in it (Luft et al.
  2022).
- **A list of theories ≠ a theoretical framework.** Naming Bourdieu,
  Foucault, and Latour in one paragraph is a reading list; a framework
  states which concepts from whom, doing what work, with what
  relationship to each other.
- **Model ≠ framework.** A diagram without the narrative argument is
  an illustration; the framework is the argued structure the diagram
  summarizes.
